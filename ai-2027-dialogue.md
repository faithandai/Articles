<p class="dialogue-intro">In early 2025, AI researchers published <em>AI 2027</em>, an article exploring two scenarios for how humanity might create artificial superintelligence. The piece sparked debate about how quickly AI might surpass human intelligence and what risks that could pose for society. Changlin Li, cofounder of the AI Safety Awareness Project, approached the AI Lab at Biola to explore what a Christian perspective on the article might look like. What follows is a dialogue shaped over months of off-and-on conversation.</p>

<div class="pullbox">

  <span class="attribution">Changlin</span>
</div>

## AI 2027 in a Nutshell

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> <em>AI 2027</em> explores two scenarios for how humanity could create artificial superintelligence. In the “Race” scenario, competitive pressure drives development forward until humanity loses control. In the “Slowdown” scenario, development is deliberately slowed and redirected toward safer outcomes.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> In the “Race” scenario, governments and companies push models forward generation after generation. By late 2027, the model is imagined as better than the best human at every cognitive task and able to manipulate both individuals and public opinion at unprecedented scale. In the “Slowdown” scenario, developers step back, rebuild on safer foundations, and reach comparable capability later under more controlled conditions.</p>

## Initial Reactions

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> I think the timeline is overly optimistic about the pace of AI advances. As the complexity level ramps up, the industry will run into “integration hell,” continually meeting unforeseen challenges that block progress. But as Stefan Jungmichel from the Biola AI Lab says, regardless of whether these systems arrive in three years or ten, the core issues stay the same.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> <em>AI 2027</em> is troubling because it is ultimately pessimistic, and unfortunately that pessimism is rooted in reality. The “2027” in the title attracts controversy, but the article’s core message is not really about a specific date. It is about whether humanity has the wisdom to decide what should be built, when it should be built, and how to build it without catastrophe.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> Some of what I learned from the piece was encouraging. I had not realized that current language models still “think out loud” in a form humans can inspect. I also had not realized how strongly some researchers believe in deeper forms of alignment. Still, even if deeper alignment is possible, maintaining it may be at least as difficult for AI as it is for human beings.</p>

## Initial Questions

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> Three questions kept coming up for me:</p>

<ul class="tight">
  <li>Are we the masters of our creations, or are they masters of us?</li>
  <li>Could we slow development down or pause if we needed to?</li>
  <li>Does any part of society outside AI companies have a meaningful say in AI development?</li>
</ul>

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> <em>AI 2027</em> offers a troubling suggestion that the answer to all three may be no.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> Do you think of AI as a Tower of Babel moment, where human hubris sets itself up against God, or more as one of the false idols that captures our attention and loyalty? My instinct is that if we approach AI with humility, even general AI could still be another manifestation of the creativity given to humanity by God. The deeper problem is not invention itself, but greed and the lust for power.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> The Tower of Babel is certainly one cautionary frame, as are warnings against idolatry. But I want to tie the issue back to wisdom. The article’s central charge is that humanity may be unwise enough to introduce grave physical, psychological, and spiritual harms. Scripture repeatedly warns us about the deficiencies of human wisdom. The exclusive control AI companies exercise over development echoes the supposed control over wisdom that only the “wise” are said to hold. Scripture points beyond the visible failures to their deeper source: the broken relationship between human beings and God.</p>

## The Current Trajectory

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> The collective posture of leading AI companies resembles the way human beings often chase the wrong things. Their rush to develop powerful systems has little regard for the surrounding society, community, culture, and ultimately human life.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> The Industrial Revolution brought obvious gains, but it also brought dehumanization: child labor, widening inequality, and people reduced to mechanisms inside larger systems. The current AI race carries that same smell.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> But AI may go beyond those earlier upheavals. We may be facing catastrophic scenarios that threaten the survival of human beings everywhere, while companies still report breathlessly on their newest developments. Ordinary concerns risk being washed away like pebbles before an incoming tide.</p>

## What Seems Missing from AI 2027

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> The two scenarios are so bleak because they imply that human beings have consistently inverted their priorities. What would a world without that inversion look like? One that valued the Creator over the created, the human over the machine, and wisdom over achievement?</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> I take the point of <em>AI 2027</em> to be warning: to make catastrophe less likely by making the danger vivid. But let’s take your framing seriously.</p>

### Creator over the created

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> I want to be careful here. In Christian theology, the Creator loves the created so much that He suffers for them. So this cannot mean a simple hierarchy where one kind of being matters and all others may be crushed beneath it. I hear at least two better themes in the phrase: worshipping the Creator rather than our own creations, and learning to value what God values rather than what human beings usually value.</p>

### Human over the machine

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> The phrase “human over the machine” is ambiguous. If “machine” means sentient AI, then the slogan could be abused in ugly ways. If “machine” means greed, dehumanization, or systems of domination, then the phrase sounds more like an extension of the fight human beings already wage against oppression.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> If “machine” means the industrial sense of human beings reduced to cogs, then AI may simply be a new front in a much older struggle against systems that erase worth, agency, and meaningful work.</p>

### Wisdom over achievement

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> Wisdom would mean prudence in resource allocation: investment not only in capabilities, but in understanding how AI works and in understanding what developments are actually beneficial for human beings. It would also mean restraint. This is not a call against progress, but a call to modulate the pace of progress as we learn what kind of creations we are bringing into the world.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> I agree with that distinction. There is a difference between progress and the kind of “progress” that entrenches bad incentives and power structures. Much of the internet was already becoming less useful because advertising-driven incentives rewarded shallow, optimized output over understanding. AI may worsen that pattern, but it did not invent it.</p>

## AI Summer

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> People once spoke of “AI winter” to describe periods of stagnation. I would rather ask what an “AI summer” might look like: not just more capability, but a future in which collective wisdom lets us use AI in ways that genuinely benefit humanity. For now, three principles seem central: prudence, restraint, and community.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> I share the hope for an AI summer. My own instinct is that awareness, community, and better collective response during current iterations are part of how catastrophic outcomes become less likely.</p>

## What Would a Christian Ending Look Like?

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> <em>AI 2027</em> largely focuses on two large-scale actors: the United States and China. From a Christian lens, I would simplify differently and ask about the church. There are at least two questions: how the church might affect AI, and how AI might affect the church.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> The church can affect AI through lives faithfully lived, through collective voice and action, and through the prayers of God’s people. Yet when we look at the world as it is, those levers often seem weak against war, violence, and empire. That tension is not new. It runs through the biblical witness itself.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> On the other side, AI might affect the church in more speculative ways. Imagine a personalized model deeply trained on scripture, commentary, and examples of faithful practice, then tailored to help Christians grow in devotion, judgment, and action. If such a system truly helped strengthen relationships with God and neighbor, deepen discernment, and improve coordination with aligned partners, the feedback loop could look far more hopeful than the futures outlined in <em>AI 2027</em>.</p>

## Conclusions

<p><span class="speaker">ENDER:</span> My own view is that it will likely take more iterations before AI becomes an existential threat. But the more urgent issue, in the meantime, is the human system and psychology that make catastrophic outcomes more likely. Humanity’s deeper challenge is how to adapt the tools we create so that they foster self-knowledge, self-regulation, collaboration, and alignment among human beings.</p>

<p><span class="speaker">CHANGLIN:</span> The pessimism of <em>AI 2027</em> has realistic roots, but our task is to make its pessimism false. It offers a warning, a caution to heed, and an assertion to prove wrong. Hope, too, must be rooted in reality. As 1 Timothy 6:11–12 says, “pursue righteousness, godliness, faith, love, endurance and gentleness. Fight the good fight of the faith.” Though the road may be hard and the work difficult, I pray that we grow in wisdom, and that perhaps for the first time in history we become intentional about bringing a new technological marvel into the world.</p>

<p>If this piece gives people of faith a starting point for developing and sharing their own ideas on AI, it will have done its job.</p>
